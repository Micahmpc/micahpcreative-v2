Introduction

Aetlas is a globally conscious funding platform for campaigns to launch products and initiatives that positively affect the planet and its people. Campaigners can build their projects, choose from different types of funding options, and grow ideas into ecocentric successes. Donors can browse through featured and/or specific projects to find products or projects they can feel passionate about, and get behind them.

Research / User Experience / Visual Design / Prototype
Problem

Crowdfunding and fundraising sites have a hard time with user trust and retention. There are issues with campaigns not being able to meet promised production quotas when they are successfully funded, and they are often abused which leads to them to become oversaturated with campaigns and deemed untrustworthy as a whole.

Solution

Aetlas seeks to set itself apart from the pack by getting back to the core of why crowdfunding was invented. To help breathe life into ideas worthy enough to become realized. It approaches trust and retention by using its brand identity and campaign building process as a filter. By removing the some of the ability to abuse this type of platform, Aetlas aims to rebuild trust around its own platform and increase retention rates by making users feel safe first, then helping them feel like they're making a difference by showing their interest and support.

I ran a SWOT analysis on a few popular crowdfunding and fundraising platforms to see where they were succeeding and where there was room to improve. This step illuminated how I could separate Aetlas from what's out there, and also gave me a few key "what not to do" points.

Personas
View Personas

After the survey, I did a number of follow-up interviews to dig deeper. I was fortunate enough to interview a few potential users who, between them, spanned the spectrum from casual/novice donor to a non-profit fundraiser to a marketer who specializes in crowdfunding and campaign building.

Flows & Architecture
View Flows & Architecture

Now that I had defined the problem, the user, and the direction, I started working out how the user would move through the site, and how it was structured. I ran a card-sort study using Optimal Workshop which gave me my architecture and sitemap. Then I started working through user-flows to solve for the MVP. This included creating a campaign, campaign management, sign up/sign in, and member and guest donor flows.

Lo-fi Wireframes
View Lo-fi Wireframes

For the interface, I started with some quick sketches and notes and began building the low fidelity version of the app. All the screens were done in XD and compiled into a working prototype in InVision. They covered sign up and sign on, project browsing with search and filter, project pages, and checkout on the donor side. The campaign host side had the projects dashboard, and project manager.

User & Preference Testing
View User Testing

I shopped the prototype around for some remote testing. I gave the participants a number of standard tasks to walk through to make the platform was understandable and simple enough, and to get some feedback. For preference testing, I did a number of A/B tests with Usability Hub for things like how to handle pagination, credit card input, and whether users wanted a dark or light UI.

Hi-fi & Prototype
Demo Prototype

For the prototype, I wanted to be as thorough as possible to ensure users demoing the product didn't get lost as I saw a bit in the lo-fi tests. That meant creating the screens for guest and member flows starting at the homepage and guiding them all the way through browse and on to completing checkout. I also made sure that all functionality was accounted for - meaning dropdowns working, clickable buttons, and menus working on all pages.

Takeaway

The concept for Aetlas takes a trust-first approach. What the research initially highlighted showed me that the idea of crowdfunding had a ton of merit, but the implementations I saw created environments without barriers to entry. I see this issue going beyond just crowdfunding - the internet itself has no barriers, and it can be both a great and terrible place. Barriers can be good and bad, you don't want to turn people away, but at the same time - in some settings like this, you also don't always want a free-for-all.

Aetlas made me very aware of designing with user-trust in mind and how that can affect your product. It raised questions, like are there vetting processes to alleviate pains potential abuse of platform - that don't deter your desired users as well? Or how can effectively deter certain users via the process?

I'm left wondering if one could somehow add non-tangible rewards for campaigners to form a sort of habit loop that further improves retention and implies improved trust if that loop continues to work. Maybe creating a follower type of system with campaigners where donors could follow you and see what your latest campaign is all about?
